Mars Exploration Rovers

NASA’s robotic explorers help us uncover Mars’ history, climate, and potential for life. Each rover is a mobile science lab built to survive the harsh, dusty terrain of the Red Planet.

Spirit Rover

Exploring Gusev Crater — 2004 to 2010

Mission Goal

Search for clues that water once existed on Mars and understand the planet's geologic history.

Launch & Landing

  • Launch: June 10, 2003
  • Landing: Gusev Crater, January 4, 2004

Major Discoveries

  • Found evidence of minerals formed in ancient water.
  • Recorded detailed panoramas of Martian landscapes.
  • Helped confirm that Mars once had a wetter environment.

Key Features

  • Six-wheel rocker-bogie suspension
  • Panoramic mast cameras
  • Rock abrasion tool
  • Solar-powered energy system

Opportunity Rover

15 Years of Discovery — 2004 to 2019

Mission Goal

Investigate clues of past water activity on Mars and study rock layers that reveal environmental history.

Launch & Landing

  • Launch: July 7, 2003
  • Landing: Meridiani Planum, January 25, 2004

Major Discoveries

  • Discovered “blueberries” — hematite spheres linked to past liquid water.
  • Found evidence of ancient groundwater systems.
  • Traveled over 28 miles — the longest distance ever driven on another world.

Key Features

  • Panoramic and microscopic cameras
  • Robotic arm with precision tools
  • Advanced navigation software

Curiosity Rover

A Nuclear-Powered Mobile Lab — 2012 to Present

Mission Goal

Major Discoveries

Key Features

  • Laser spectrometer (“ChemCam”)
  • High-resolution science cameras
